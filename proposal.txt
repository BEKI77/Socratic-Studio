                                   INTRODUCTION

The integration of abstract theoretical knowledge with practical applications continues to present
significant challenges in STEM education, particularly in physics and mathematics. Research
work demonstrates that students frequently struggle to connect mathematical formalism with
physical intuition, resulting in limited problem-solving transferability and conceptual
understanding [1]. The usual traditional educational approaches and existing digital learning
platforms fail to provide adequate personalized scaffolding, offering standardized content that
cannot adapt to individual learning trajectories or domain-specific contexts.

This project aims to bridge these limitations through an SLM-based Socratic Assistant
implementing Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architecture, building upon recent
advancements in educational AI systems [2]. The system constructs a dynamic knowledge base
from individual students' academic materials including lecture notes, textbooks, and
problem-solving histories, enabling context-aware tutoring that emulates the adaptive support
mechanisms of human experts [3]. By embedding established pedagogical frameworks that
prioritize conceptual understanding and real-world applications, the assistant delivers
personalized guidance across fundamental undergraduate topics, transforming passive content
consumption into an active learning partnership that cultivates both technical proficiency and
practical intuition.

1.1 Background of the Study

Here are the key technical concepts involved in the project, explained to create the contextual
background necessary to understand the project.

Static vs. Dynamic Knowledge Repositories
Static repositories are centralized storages for archival and retrieval purposes. They require users
to know exactly what they are searching for, lack contextual understanding of queries, cannot
adapt to individual learning needs, and provide no guidance on how to apply retrieved
information. Dynamic KM systems address these shortcomings through intelligent query
interpretation, personalized content recommendation, adaptive learning pathways, and
integration of multiple knowledge sources.

AI-Enhanced Knowledge Management Architecture
Modern AI-powered KM systems incorporate several key components: semantic understanding
engines that interpret user intent, knowledge graphs that map relationships between concepts,
recommendation algorithms that suggest relevant materials, and feedback loops that
continuously improve system performance based on user interactions.

These KM systems can also be implemented for educational purposes as well. The usual method
of education has a slow pace of development and is characterized with deprived smart KM
system. Studies show AI based KM implementation in the higher education sector has a
promising result.

Adaptive Learning Technologies
Current adaptive learning systems employ various approaches: rule-based systems that follow
predetermined branching logic, Bayesian knowledge tracing that models student understanding
probabilistically, item response theory that adjusts difficulty based on performance, and deep
learning models that identify patterns in student behavior. Each approach has strengths and
limitations in terms of accuracy, interpretability, and computational requirements.




                                                                                                  2
RAG Architecture Fundamentals
Retrieval-Augmented Generation represents a hybrid approach combining information retrieval
with text generation. Unlike pure language models that rely solely on parameters learned during
training, RAG systems retrieve relevant information from external knowledge bases before
generating responses. This architecture consists of a retriever component that finds relevant
documents, an encoder that processes retrieved information, and a generator that produces
coherent responses grounded in retrieved content. Recent systematic surveys summarize RAG‚Äôs
technical components, deployment patterns, and practical deployment challenges, reinforcing
RAG as the recommended architecture for knowledge-intensive tutoring systems. A
comprehensive review of RAG explains core pipeline stages‚Äîchunking, dense embedding,
(re)ranking, and generator fusion strategies (early/late/weighted)‚Äîand formalizes RAG as
marginalizing generator outputs across retrieved documents, which explains how grounding
reduces hallucinations and allows knowledge updates without retraining [6].

Vector Database Technologies
Vector databases provide efficient storage and retrieval of embeddings. Technologies like FAISS,
Pinecone, Weaviate, and Milvus offer different trade-offs between speed, accuracy, and
scalability. Key features include approximate nearest neighbor search for fast retrieval, filtering
capabilities to constrain searches, and hybrid search combining semantic and keyword
approaches.

                             LITERATURE REVIEW

2.1 Knowledge Management Systems in Education

Companies rely on documentation to expand and utilize their knowledge base. They can achieve
this in two methods, either with static repositories or dynamic Knowledge Management (KM)
systems. To meet today‚Äôs standard, the second option is most preferred. There are simple
dynamic KMs that have been around for a while but empowering them with AI boosts the
efficiency. This is a suitable implementation of AI in knowledge management [4].

Evolution of Knowledge Management Systems

The trajectory of KM systems has progressed through three distinct generations. First-generation
systems (1990s-2000s) focused on document management and basic search functionality,
essentially serving as digital filing cabinets. Second-generation systems (2000s-2010s)
introduced taxonomies, metadata tagging, and collaborative features, enabling better
organization and knowledge sharing. Third-generation systems (2010s-present) leverage artificial
intelligence, machine learning, and natural language processing to provide intelligent,
context-aware knowledge delivery.

Challenges of implementing AI in existing system
The implementation of AI in the knowledge management system face many challenges. One of
the main challenges in implementing AI into such knowledge management systems is the
question of privacy. The data used to train these models may involve personal and organizational
data. There is also the concern of biasing in the training process. If the training is biased, it
compromises the integrity and reliability of the implemented model to do its task.

As a new system, integration of AI into an organizational system must align with the
organizational value and principles. Defying these values results in resistance to embrace the
technological significance of the new system. Historically, new technologies of their era also


                                                                                               9
have technological challenges regarding the available resources, security, robustness and
complexity.

All these concerns regarding AI implementation into an existing system can be classified into
three major groups. Technological challenges: includes factors like data quality and availability,
scalability, transparency, reliability and robustness, security and privacy, and algorithm
complexity, organizational challenges: involves factors like resistance to change, lack of
expertise, interdepartmental collaboration, and budget constraints and ethical challenges:
encompassing the factors bias, accountability, privacy, transparency, and lack of job security [5].


2.2 The Learning Crisis in STEM Education

Quantifying the Problem
The integration of abstract theoretical knowledge with practical applications continues to present
significant challenges in STEM education, particularly in physics and mathematics. Current
research demonstrates that students frequently struggle to connect mathematical formalism with
physical intuition, resulting in limited problem-solving transferability and conceptual
understanding.

Wieman's comprehensive study revealed alarming statistics: only 30% of students retain core
concepts one semester after instruction, 65% of students cannot apply learned formulas to novel
problems, 45% report feeling "lost" during lectures despite attending regularly, and 70% rely on
memorization rather than understanding. These findings suggest fundamental flaws in the usual
educational approaches.

Cognitive Load Theory and STEM Learning
Cognitive load theory provides insight into why students struggle with complex STEM topics.
Working memory has limited capacity, and when instructional materials impose excessive
cognitive load, learning suffers. STEM subjects often present high intrinsic complexity (inherent
difficulty of the material), extraneous load (poor instructional design), and insufficient germane
load (cognitive resources devoted to schema construction and automation).



                                                                                                 10
The Transfer Problem
One of the most significant challenges in STEM education is the transfer of learning‚Äîthe ability
to apply knowledge learned in one context to new situations. Research indicates that students
often develop "brittle knowledge" that works only for problems identical to those practiced. They
struggle to recognize when a concept applies to a novel scenario, cannot adapt solution strategies
to new problem types, and fail to connect related concepts across different courses.

Misconceptions and Conceptual Change
Students enter STEM courses with pre-existing mental models, often based on everyday
experience, that conflict with scientific understanding. These misconceptions are remarkably
resistant to change. Traditional instruction that simply presents correct information rarely
succeeds in overcoming deeply held incorrect beliefs. Effective learning requires explicitly
confronting misconceptions, providing cognitive conflict that challenges existing beliefs, and
offering alternative frameworks that better explain observations.


2.3. Personalized Learning and Adaptive Systems

The usual educational approaches and existing digital learning platforms fail to provide adequate
personalized scaffolding, offering standardized content that cannot adapt to individual learning
trajectories or domain-specific contexts.

The Case for Personalization
Educational research consistently demonstrates that personalized instruction significantly
outperforms one-size-fits-all approaches. Students learn at different rates, require different
amounts of practice, benefit from different explanation styles, and have varying levels of prior
knowledge. Personalized learning systems can adjust content difficulty dynamically, provide
additional examples when needed, skip material already mastered, and adapt explanation style to
student preferences.




                                                                                               11
Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS)
Intelligent Tutoring Systems represent a mature approach to personalized education. Successful
ITS implementations share common features: detailed student models tracking knowledge and
skills, expert models encoding domain knowledge, pedagogical models determining instructional
strategies, and user interfaces facilitating natural interaction. Studies show well-designed ITS can
approach the effectiveness of human one-on-one tutoring.

The review also shows that targeted (guided) use of generative AI yields better learning
outcomes than unguided frequent usage, indicating that personalization must be coupled with
scaffolding on how to interact with the system. This suggests the personal assistant should
include onboarding / prompt templates and scaffolded workflows (e.g., ‚Äúexplain concept‚Äù,
‚ÄúSocratic hint‚Äù, ‚Äúworked numerical example‚Äù) rather than leave all prompting to novice users
[7].

Limitations of Current Platforms
Despite advances, most educational technology platforms exhibit significant shortcomings. They
offer limited content coverage restricted to specific topics, lack integration with students'
personal materials, provide superficial feedback without deep explanation, cannot handle
open-ended questions effectively, and fail to develop metacognitive skills and learning strategies.


2.4 Retrieval-Augmented

Generation: A Paradigm Shift
This project addresses these limitations through a SLM-based Socratic Assistant implementing
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architecture, building upon recent advancements in
educational AI systems.

Advantages Over Pure Language Models
RAG systems address critical limitations of standard language models. They reduce
hallucinations by grounding responses in factual sources, enable easy knowledge updates without
retraining, provide transparency through source attribution, and scale to large knowledge bases


                                                                                                 12
efficiently. For educational applications, these advantages are particularly valuable as accuracy
and verifiability are paramount.

RAG in Knowledge-Intensive Tasks
Lewis et al. demonstrated RAG's superiority in knowledge-intensive NLP tasks, showing
improvements of 10-20% over comparable language-only models on question answering
benchmarks. The system performs particularly well on tasks requiring factual accuracy,
integration of multiple information sources, and handling of rare or specialized knowledge.

Educational RAG Implementations
Applying RAG to education requires specific adaptations. Educational RAG systems must
handle multi-modal content (text, equations, diagrams), maintain pedagogical appropriateness in
responses, balance accuracy with appropriate difficulty level, and support progressive disclosure
of information. Recent implementations have shown promising results in mathematics tutoring,
science education, and programming instruction.

Technical Challenges in Educational RAG
Several technical challenges arise when implementing RAG for education: chunking strategies
must preserve conceptual coherence, retrieval must consider pedagogical relevance not just
semantic similarity, generation must follow Socratic principles rather than direct answer
provision, and the system must handle mathematical notation and equations effectively.


2.5 The Socratic Method and AI-Powered Tutoring

The system constructs a dynamic knowledge base from individual students' academic materials
including lecture notes, textbooks, and problem-solving histories, enabling context-aware
tutoring that emulates the adaptive support mechanisms of human experts.

Historical Foundation of Socratic Teaching
The Socratic method, developed by the ancient Greek philosopher Socrates, emphasizes learning
through questioning rather than direct instruction. This approach guides students to discover
knowledge independently, develops critical thinking skills, exposes gaps in understanding, and

                                                                                              13
promotes deeper, more durable learning. Modern educational psychology has validated the
effectiveness of this approach, particularly for developing higher-order thinking skills.

Cognitive Science Foundations
The Knowledge-Learning-Instruction (KLI) framework proposed by Koedinger et al. provides
theoretical grounding for effective tutoring. This framework distinguishes between different
types of knowledge: declarative knowledge (facts and concepts), procedural knowledge (how to
perform tasks), and conditional knowledge (when to apply procedures). Effective instruction
must address all three types and facilitate their integration.

Desirable Difficulties and Productive Struggle
Contemporary learning science emphasizes that some difficulty during learning is beneficial.
"Desirable   difficulties"   that require    effortful   retrieval, generation of answers, and
connection-making lead to stronger learning than passive review. Socratic tutoring naturally
incorporates these principles by requiring students to actively construct understanding rather than
passively receive information.

Implementation in AI Systems
Implementing Socratic tutoring in AI systems requires sophisticated prompt engineering and
dialogue management. The system must ask appropriate questions that guide without revealing
answers, recognize when students are genuinely stuck versus productively struggling, provide
hints at appropriate levels of specificity, and adjust questioning strategy based on student
responses. Multi-turn dialogue capabilities and context maintenance are essential.

Prompt Engineering for Socratic Dialogue
Effective Socratic AI tutors employ multi-layered prompts that establish the tutoring role and
pedagogical approach, incorporate retrieved context from student materials, structure the
dialogue to guide discovery, and include safeguards against providing direct answers. Prompt
design must balance guidance with autonomy, ensuring students remain engaged without
becoming frustrated.



                                                                                                14
A controlled classroom study validates a constructivist prompt-engineering approach called the
Constructivist Inquiry-Based Learning Prompting (CILP) framework, where students were
trained to craft, refine, and critique prompts when using ChatGPT as a supplemental tutor. The
intervention (n=120, quasi-experimental) demonstrated statistically significant gains in
conceptual understanding and reductions in qualitative misconceptions (e.g., entropy, internal
energy) when ChatGPT was used in a guided, inquiry-based manner ‚Äî though
quantitative/numerical problem-solving gains were limited. This supports embedding explicit
prompt-crafting and metacognitive reflection into AI tutoring workflows [7].


2.6 Vector Databases and Semantic Search Technology

The usual information retrieval based on keyword matching suffers from vocabulary mismatch
problems. Students may phrase questions differently than how information appears in source
materials, synonyms and related terms are not recognized, and conceptual relationships between
topics are ignored. Semantic search addresses these limitations by representing text based on
meaning rather than exact words.

Vector Embeddings and Semantic Space
Modern semantic search relies on vector embeddings‚Äîdense numerical representations of text in
high-dimensional space. These embeddings are learned by neural networks trained on large text
corpora, capturing semantic relationships such that similar concepts have similar vector
representations. In this semantic space, mathematical operations correspond to meaningful
relationships: similar vectors represent similar concepts, vector arithmetic can capture analogies
(e.g., "king" - "man" + "woman" ‚âà "queen"), and distance metrics quantify semantic similarity.

Embedding Models for Educational Content
Choosing appropriate embedding models is crucial for educational applications. General-purpose
models like Sentence-BERT provide strong baseline performance, domain-specific models
fine-tuned on scientific text better capture technical terminology, and multi-modal models can
handle equations and diagrams alongside text. Recent research explores embedding models
specifically optimized for educational content, incorporating pedagogical relationships between
concepts. The review highlights practical chunking strategies specific to educational content
                                                                                                 15
(semantic chunking that respects topic boundaries, controlled overlap to retain continuity, and
metadata enrichment) and recommends hybrid retrieval (dense + BM25) plus re-ranking for
increased precision in high-stakes domains. These findings directly inform index construction for
student-uploaded materials. [6]

Chunking Strategies for Educational Content
Effective RAG systems require careful text chunking‚Äîdividing documents into segments for
embedding and retrieval. Educational content presents unique challenges: preserving complete
explanations and derivations, maintaining context for mathematical expressions, balancing
granularity (small chunks for precision vs. large chunks for context), and handling hierarchical
structure (sections, subsections, examples).

Research suggests optimal strategies include semantic chunking that respects topic boundaries,
overlapping chunks (10-15%) to prevent context loss, metadata enrichment with section titles
and topics, and adaptive chunk sizing based on content density.


2.7 Multi-Document Integration and Context Synthesis

The Challenge of Fragmented Knowledge
Students' learning materials are inherently fragmented across multiple sources: lecture notes
from different sessions, textbook chapters, supplementary readings, problem sets, and personal
notes. Traditional search returns individual fragments without synthesizing information across
sources. Effective learning requires integrating these fragments into coherent understanding.

Cross-Document Reasoning
Advanced RAG systems must perform cross-document reasoning: identifying complementary
information across sources, resolving contradictions or different perspectives, synthesizing
comprehensive answers from multiple fragments, and tracking provenance to attribute
information to sources. This capability is particularly valuable for complex topics covered
differently in various materials.




                                                                                                16
Context Window Management
Language models have limited context windows‚Äîthe amount of text they can process
simultaneously. When retrieved content exceeds this limit, sophisticated strategies are needed:
ranking retrieved chunks by relevance, compressing or summarizing less critical information,
iterative refinement where initial responses are enhanced with additional context, and
hierarchical processing that first identifies relevant documents then focuses on specific sections.


2.8 Evaluation Metrics for Educational AI Systems

Technical Performance Metrics
Evaluating RAG systems requires multiple technical metrics. Retrieval accuracy measures
whether the system finds relevant information: precision (proportion of retrieved chunks that are
relevant), recall (proportion of relevant chunks that are retrieved), and Mean Reciprocal Rank
(MRR) assessing ranking quality. Generation quality evaluates the produced responses: factual
accuracy against source materials, coherence and fluency of explanations, and faithfulness to
retrieved content without hallucination.

Pedagogical Effectiveness Metrics
Beyond technical performance, educational systems must be evaluated on learning outcomes:
conceptual understanding measured through pre- / post-testing, transfer ability to solve novel
problems, retention over time, and metacognitive development including learning strategies and
self-assessment accuracy.

The RAG survey further synthesizes recommended evaluation axes‚Äîretrieval metrics
(precision/recall/MRR),     generation     faithfulness   (fact-check    /   hallucination    rates),
latency/compute efficiency, and task-specific pedagogical outcomes ‚Äî recommending mixed
technical + pedagogical evaluation for educational RAG systems. These metrics should be used
to evaluate both your retriever (how often it finds the right passage) and generator (how
accurately it explains or scaffolds learning) [6].

User Experience Metrics
Student satisfaction and engagement are critical for adoption: perceived usefulness and ease of
use, time spent with the system, types of questions asked, and qualitative feedback on
explanation quality.


2.9 Ethical Considerations and Limitations

Academic Integrity Concerns
AI tutoring systems must carefully balance support with academic integrity. Systems should
guide learning without completing assignments, encourage understanding over answer-seeking,
and include safeguards against misuse. Transparent communication about appropriate use cases
is essential.

Bias and Fairness
AI systems can perpetuate biases present in training data or source materials. Educational AI
must be evaluated for equitable performance across different student populations, representation
of diverse perspectives, and accessibility for students with disabilities.

Privacy and Data Security
Student data is highly sensitive. Systems must implement robust security measures, obtain
informed consent, provide transparency about data usage, and comply with regulations like
FERPA and GDPR. The review explicitly flags privacy and security as open challenges for RAG
when indexing user-owned or proprietary data, and points to emerging directions
(privacy-preserving RAG, federated vector DBs, access control layers) that must be considered
when building systems that index student notes. For a personal assistant that indexes student
documents, implementing privacy controls, consent workflows, and possible on-device or
federated indexing will be necessary to mitigate risk [6].

Limitations of AI Tutoring
AI systems cannot fully replace human instructors. They lack true understanding and may miss
subtle student difficulties, cannot provide emotional support and motivation, and may struggle

                                                                                             18
with highly creative or open-ended problems. AI tutoring is best viewed as a complement to, not
replacement for, human instruction.


2.10 Related Works, Gap Analysis and Project Contribution

Current Landscape Limitations
Despite significant advances in educational technology and AI, substantial gaps remain in
available solutions. Existing systems typically focus on single domains with preloaded content,
lack integration with students' personal materials, provide limited personalization based on
individual learning trajectories, offer superficial feedback without deep Socratic guidance, and
fail to synthesize information across multiple sources.

While CILP and other generative-AI tutoring interventions demonstrate clear benefits for
conceptual understanding, they also expose key limitations: generative models without retrieval
grounding can struggle with numerical accuracy and with citing sources. This underscores our
choice to use a RAG-based assistant that fuses the dialogic strengths of LLMs (Socratic
questioning, conceptual scaffolding) with retrieval grounding (student materials, worked
examples and authoritative sources) to reduce hallucinations and improve numeric/derivation
accuracy [7].

This Project's Unique Contribution
This Personal SLM-based Socratic Assistant addresses these gaps through several innovations:
personal knowledge base construction from student-uploaded materials, RAG architecture
ensuring factual accuracy grounded in student's own content, Socratic tutoring approach
promoting deep understanding, multi-document synthesis for comprehensive answers, and
context-aware responses considering student's learning history.

The system represents a significant advance toward truly personalized, adaptive STEM education
that bridges the gap between theoretical knowledge and practical application, transforming
passive content consumption into active learning partnerships.

Model Selection and Computational Optimization
After a comparative analysis of available Small Language Models (SLMs), this project selects
Microsoft‚Äôs Phi-3.5 Mini.

Unlike general-purpose models trained on internet scrapings, the Phi series is trained specifically
on "textbook-quality" synthetic data. This training methodology results in superior performance
on reasoning capabilities and STEM-related logic tasks, making it uniquely improved for an
educational tutoring assistant.

The model supports a 128k token context window, allowing the system to ingest and analyze
entire chapters or lengthy lecture notes in a single inference pass, ensuring comprehensive
answer synthesis.

To balance performance with resource efficiency, the model will be deployed using 4-bit
Quantization (GGUF format). This optimization allows for edge deployment, enabling the
system to perform low-latency inference on standard computing hardware without reliance on
external cloud computing resources or extensive training periods.

For the semantic retrieval component, the system utilizes the bge-small-en-v1.5 model. This
model was selected for its high placement on the MTEB (Massive Text Embedding Benchmark),
offering a high-fidelity vector representation of technical engineering terminology while
maintaining a compact computational footprint.

By the end, this project aims to achieve the following outcomes:

5.1 Technical Performance

The system is expected to demonstrate superior performance in knowledge-intensive tasks
compared to standard language models by reducing fabrication rates. By retrieving relevant
document segments before generating answers, the fidelity of the responses to the source
material is expected to increase [2].

The search mechanism is expected to successfully overcome semantic discontinuity. Through the

one-on-one tutoring [3].

reduction in common misconceptions in STEM topics, as opposed to the unguided use of
generative AI [7].

The final result will be a working Proof of Concept (PoC) capable of ingesting PDF/LaTeX
documents, indexing them into a Vector Database, and allowing a user to interact with this

                                      CONCLUSION

This project was initiated to address the persistent disconnect between abstract theoretical
knowledge and practical application in STEM education. Research highlights that traditional
educational approaches often fail to facilitate the transfer of learning, leaving students relying on
rote memorization rather than deep conceptual understanding [1]. The developed Personal
Knowledge SLM-based Assistant serves as a strategic intervention to bridge this gap, offering a
specialized tool that transforms passive content consumption into an active, inquiry-based
learning partnership.

To achieve this, the system leverages a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architecture,
moving beyond the limitations of standard generative models. By grounding AI responses in a
dynamic knowledge base constructed from the student‚Äôs own academic materials, the system
mitigates the risks of unverified outputs and semantic hallucination commonly associated with
Large Language Models [2]. This ensures that the assistance provided is, not only technically
accurate but, also contextually aligned with the specific curriculum and lecture notes the student
is currently studying.

Beyond technical accuracy, the project integrates established pedagogical frameworks to foster
cognitive development. By implementing an inquiry-based tutoring model, the assistant is
designed to guide users through reasoning processes rather than providing immediate solutions.
This method aligns with cognitive science principles, promoting constructive cognitive effort and
helping students identify and correct misconceptions through dialogic scaffolding [7]. This
approach addresses the limitations of generic educational platforms that often lack the
personalization required to support individual learning trajectories.

Ultimately, this project represents a significant shift from static archival repositories to dynamic,
AI-enhanced Knowledge Management systems [4]. By successfully combining semantic search
technology with educational theory, the proposed solution offers a viable pathway toward truly
personalized education. It demonstrates that AI can serve as a capability amplifier for students,

transforming the solitary act of studying into an interactive, context-aware partnership that
effectively prepares them for the complexities of real-world engineering challenges.
